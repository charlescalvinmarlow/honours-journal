Evaluation of the Source / Author

Langdon Winner is an academic, who began working in the 70s and was largely focused on political theory. This source was written in 1980, so the topic has naturally been developed and considered more deeply since then, but it still makes interesting arguments on technology with broad strokes. However, I often ponder how well our concerns and challenges with technology mirror generations past grappling with the same thing, and this paper discusses railroads and tomato harvesters with much the same language used to critique AI today.

Summary

Winner discusses whether artifacts, or technologies, intrinsically have politics or require certain kinds of social order. He discusses two potential answers; the first is that some technologies are inextricable from certain types of social order. The other is that some are flexible and are only strongly compatible, but not limited to, certain political structures.

Interpretation / analysis

I found myself drawn to the words Winner was putting before me, but also quite challenged to fully comprehend what I'm reading. It felt like some of his points and questions still hold true such as "Scarcely a new invention comes along that someone does not proclaim it is the salvation of a free society", with AI being our current "salvation." But the question I find myself asking is also addressed later on by Winner; "What, after all, does modern technology make possible or necessary in political life?"

Winner seems to discuss technology as a method of domination by those that control it. In particular in his time, managerial structures of corporatised technologies were a hot topic. And these society wide systems of technology that require tiered management create an idea of what society should be. We understand that you can't run a nationwide power grid as a rag tag band of individuals. Winner offers a quote from a report "The patterns of authority that work effectively in the corporation become for businessmen "the desirable model against which to compare political and economic relationships in the rest of society."" but admits this is not a proven fact.
