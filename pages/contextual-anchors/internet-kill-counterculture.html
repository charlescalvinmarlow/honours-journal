Something Material Honours Journal

The Internet Didn't kill counterculture but you won't find it on Instagram

Caroline Busta

return

Evaluation of the Source / Author

Caroline Busta is a Berlin based writer and editor, who has written for multiple art journals (in New York and Berlin) and since founded her own media "channel and community" called New Models. She has been working and writing in this space since at least 2008.

Summary

In this article Busta makes the argument that counterculture does not look like it did when the term first appeared. You may see people dressed like punks or protesting or performing anti-establishment art online, but, according to Busta, this no longer represents a *counter* to culture. Her reasoning is the systems and platforms that are typically used to share and communicate on the internet are "a massively lucrative space of capitalization, [which] profits off the personal expression and political conflict of its users." In performing your values on instagram, facebook, youtube etc, you are benefitting those platforms more than any critique can perform change. Busta describes the tech hegemony, and "the complexity of global crises" as something that cannot be so simply countered like the punks of old, and so counterculture as we know it has changed.

One example she provides is the idea of anonymity or pseudonymity. If you divest yourself from your real name, your real face and identity, you aren't performing for the system to gain from.

Interpretation / analysis

I found Busta's ideas interesting, and generally agreed with her. It is so difficult to go on instagram or youtube and trust anyone is genuine in what they are trying to communicate, whether that is a cooking video, or a healthy choice, or in a critique of art, film or anything else. It makes sense that this is a consequence of the system and what the "Tech hegemony" has intentionally placed value on through the eyes of profit. When Busta talks of the system's "maximize engagement while obfuscating responsibility for the social and environmental damae it wreaks.", all I can think of is Cliff Kuang in User Friendly when he discusses the history of addiction in capitalism, and how now engagement is the most profitable addiction. How do you create a counterculture to the addiction of engagement? I suppose Busta is right in that you have little choice but to disengage.
