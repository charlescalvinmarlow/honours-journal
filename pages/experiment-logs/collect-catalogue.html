Aims

I wanted to explore the use of language in the tech arena, and so decided to gather some screenshots of taglines i found on various AI companies websites (Gemini, Claude, OpenAI, xAI etc). I think a lot of this language varies from fluff to straight up deception and psyop levels, but I wanted to try and present the words in a way that makes the viewer consider the language and what it means, what the words are actually doing outside of their original context. Perhaps this will reveal something about the nature of the marketing language and its influence on us.

Precedents / Context

I just want to get more in the habit of making stuff, so I admittedly didn't search for projects or visual precedents and just rolled with the idea as they came but I have been reading about this area: We need to talk about how we talk about AI by Emily M. Bender, Nanna Inie. In this article Bender and Inie discuss how much of the language of AI is in terms of cognition and emotions, how it makes it sound like its thinking and how it is its own entity that the corporation can't entirely be blamed for.

Process / Methods

To collect the quotes I just explored the marketing pages of the big AI companies, there was plenty of material but I admittedly was selective in choosing short snappy statements that say a lot but also very little. I screenshot them and collated them on a figma file to look at as a whole.

As I was exploring I found this crazy interactive element on the xAI website that was selling itself as some kind of sci-fi deity, and began to wonder what would these words look like with different visuals?

So I decided to try and create some "scenes" to display all the different quotes in to see if it gives them a different feeling, if it makes the words meaning change. The idea was to make an interactive web scene with vector illustrations that animate, so I created some illustrations in Illustrator. I then created a simple website that allows to scroll through the scenes and flick through some of the quotes.

Reflection on Action

After designing the artwork, which I had made vector based because it is suitable for the web (low file sizes, animatable etc) I was looking at it and realised these factors that make it suitable for the web also make it feel like it is too closely speaking the language of the web that I wanted to challenge (or at least investigate) so I think trying some different styles or seeing how vector illustrations can be pushed to a different sort of language would be beneficial to this project.

When trying to make a simple web prototype I came across the challenge of working with code, there are always many little intricacies involved in creating the things that we use and interact with daily. It is challenging to work through these, and as it is impossible to avoid the endless hype on the internet around LLMs and their ability to code I would occasionally ask them for help. I must admit the feedback is quite impressive, it would look at a small snippet and say oh, this function name you have created is the same as a default function that exists so it is creating a conflict that the code cannot handle. I would never be able to work this out without someone who knew that default function exists. While it can create things for you, I think as I am approaching this from a visual perspective, if I don't also understand it, I can't edit it to do what I actually designed in the way I designed it, so I find it more useful to try the thing and when I'm truly stuck looking at something that I can't possibly progress with, to ask for help. However, this doesn't make it an experience without a sense of unease.

Once I had a basic working prototype, I found myself drawn to the scene with the flowers. Some of the language juxtaposed to a scene of flowers seemed to asking a more interesting question about knowledge, teamwork, experts, or whatever else the quotes I had gathered were discussing. What if we spoke about nature as we did AI? What else do we not consider that could be interesting to speak of this way?
